Real decentralization begins when your people can say “no” to you to protect the system you built together. In Kamma Township, I attempted to offer an installment plan to a customer, only to be publicly blocked by my Zone Manager, Chit Kaung. This loss of face was my proudest moment as a leader, proving the team had moved from blind obedience to radical ownership of company policy.



## The Unexpected Oasis in the Dry Zone
Kamma Township sits in the Tayet District of the Magway Region. It is a place of dusty plains where the sun makes you feel like you are standing on the edge of a desert. But when the waters retreat and the rivers step back, the soil tells another story. This part of Magway becomes an unlikely oasis. Rich in nutrients carried by the floods, the soil needs no chemical fertilizers. 

Farmers here grow the country’s best onions and sesame. They trust the land. They know the timing of the water and the weight of the sun. I was traveling with my Zone Manager, Chit Kaung, who leads our sales team across eight townships. Over the past year, his zone had become one of the top-performing in the country. I wanted to see why.

## Giving Up Control
Last year, I made a decision that could have easily gone wrong. I dismantled our centralized system. No more top-down approvals. No regional offices. No field supervisors holding clipboards. I gave full autonomy to each zone. This included the power to hire, fire, and set their own rules for their territories. I wanted to see if the managers were actually leading or just following a manual.

## "Boss, I have to hold the line"
We were at a farm, and I stepped in. I suggested to the farmer that we could offer an installment plan. It was a move to close the deal, but Chit Kaung stopped me immediately. 

He did not wait for a private moment. He told me right there: “Boss, I hope you’re not upset. I had to hold the line. Since you gave us freedom to run our zones, I can’t make exceptions. I told my guys no one could sell on installment, and they’re watching me. If I bend the rule—even for you—it all breaks.”

I had lost face in front of the reps. That night, the fan in my small hotel room felt like it was spinning over a different man. My authority had been checked by the very discipline I preached.

## The Foundation Worked
As we sat by the roadside reflecting later, I realized I had reached my goal. I was no longer needed. The team had built a culture that respected discipline without fear. Chit Kaung was not being rebellious. He was being honorable. 

He protected the health of his zone against the person who gave him the power to run it. The system did not need a supervisor to protect it because the culture held the ground. That day, I lost face, but I gained the proof that my team was truly independent and unshakable.

## FAQ: People Also Ask

**What does decentralized leadership look like in the field?**
It is the removal of top-down approvals. Local managers receive full authority over hiring, territory rules, and operational decisions.

**How do you know if your sales culture is self-sustaining?**
The culture is self-sustaining when team members enforce agreed policies even when senior leadership tries to bypass them for a quick result.

**Why is Kamma Township significant for sales strategy?**
Kamma represents a market where regional knowledge and policy consistency are more valuable than executive interference or centralized discounting.
